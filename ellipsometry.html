Ellipsometry

What is Ellipsometry?

Ellipsometry is an optical characterization technique that measures changes in the polarization state of light upon reflection from a material surface. Its high sensitivity originates from the precise determination of the relative phase change between polarized light components.

Why Is Ellipsometry So Powerful?

The sensitivity of ellipsometry arises from measuring the relative phase difference between the reflected polarization components rather than absolute intensity.

What Is Measured?

Ellipsometry measures the complex ellipsometric ratio:

$$ \rho = \frac{r_p}{r_s} = \tan(\Psi)e^{i\Delta} $$

Here, $r_p$ and $r_s$ are the complex Fresnel reflection coefficients for p- and s-polarized light. The experimentally measured quantities are:

  • $\Psi$ — amplitude ratio
  • $\Delta$ — phase difference
The ellipsometric ratio $\rho$ is a complex quantity containing both amplitude and phase information, which makes ellipsometry extremely sensitive to thin films and interfaces.

What Parameters Can Be Determined?

The ellipsometric ratio $\rho$ is related to the Fresnel equations, allowing the extraction of a wide range of optical and material properties:

  • Optical constants ($n$, $k$, $\varepsilon_1$, $\varepsilon_2$)
  • Thin film thickness
  • Surface and interfacial roughness
  • Alloy composition
  • Crystallinity
  • Optical anisotropy
  • Depth profiles
  • Growth rates

Mathematical Theory

For a non-conducting, non-dispersive medium, Maxwell’s equations lead to the electromagnetic wave equation. A plane-wave solution can be written as:

$$ \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r},t) = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}-\omega t)} $$

where the complex refractive index is defined as:

$$ \tilde{n} = n + ik $$

Jones Vectors and Jones Matrices

In the absence of space charges ($\rho = 0$) and assuming an isotropic material, the electric field components lie in the plane perpendicular to the propagation direction. The field can be expressed using s- and p-polarization components:

$$ \mathbf{E} = \begin{bmatrix} E_p \\ E_s \end{bmatrix} $$

This two-component representation is known as the Jones vector. Optical elements are described using 2×2 Jones matrices:

$$ \mathbf{E}_{out} = J \mathbf{E}_{in} $$

The diagonal elements describe changes in amplitude and phase of the s and p components, while the off-diagonal elements represent coupling between them.

Polarization States

Linear Polarization

In linear polarization, the s and p components have the same phase:

$$ \Delta = 0 $$

Circular Polarization

In circular polarization, the components have equal magnitude and a phase difference of $\pm \pi/2$:

$$ |E_p| = |E_s|,\quad \Delta = \pm \frac{\pi}{2} $$

Elliptical Polarization

In elliptical polarization, the components have unequal magnitudes and a nonzero phase difference:

$$ |E_p| \neq |E_s|,\quad \Delta \neq 0 $$

Definition of $\Psi$ and $\Delta$

$$ \tan(\Psi) = \left|\frac{r_p}{r_s}\right|,\quad \Delta = \arg(r_p) - \arg(r_s) $$

Once $\rho$ is measured, optical properties can be extracted by fitting Fresnel models to the experimental data.

Optical Ellipsometry Setup

In rotating analyzer ellipsometry:

  • Unpolarized light passes through a rotating polarizer
  • The beam reflects from the sample
  • A rotating analyzer modulates the reflected signal
  • Lock-in detection extracts $\Psi$ and $\Delta$

Conclusion

Ellipsometry provides complete information about thin films and interfaces through precise polarization measurements combined with Fresnel-based modeling.
